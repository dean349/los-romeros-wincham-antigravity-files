## PART 1 — THE FACTUAL ISSUE

### 1.1 Who Owns the Sale Proceeds?

The Spanish property sold on 20 March 2026 was held by **Los Romeros Limited**, not by Philip Harrison personally. It follows as a matter of elementary UK company law that the sale proceeds (net of the 3% AEAT retention of ~**€9,450** on a €315,000 sale) were at all times the property of the company — not of its directors or shareholders.

Under UK company law, a company is a separate legal entity (*Salomon v A Salomon & Co Ltd [1897] AC 22*). Its assets belong to the company. A shareholder or director has no proprietary right to those assets until they are lawfully distributed — either by dividend, director's salary, or (in this case) a capital distribution via Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL).

### 1.2 What Should Have Happened

A competent company accountant and company secretary advising a client on the sale of a UK Limited Company's overseas property would, as a matter of course, have:

1. **Advised the client** that all sale proceeds belong to Los Romeros Limited as a corporate entity
2. **Ensured a corporate bank account** was opened in the name of *Los Romeros Limited* before completion
3. **Instructed the Spanish notary or gestoria** to wire the net sale proceeds to that corporate account
4. **Confirmed in writing** that Philip Harrison could make no personal withdrawals until the MVL was complete
5. **Advised that any informal receipt of funds** would be treated by HMRC as either an illegal dividend or a Director's Loan, destroying the preferred CGT treatment available via the MVL route

### 1.3 What Appears to Have Happened

Rather than following that process, Wincham/Adrem:

- Did **not** advise Philip Harrison to open a corporate bank account for Los Romeros Limited
- Directed or permitted the Spanish notary to wire the sale proceeds to **Philip Harrison's personal bank account**
- Did not warn Philip Harrison that receiving these funds personally (before MVL) constituted an unlawful act from a tax and corporate governance perspective

## PART 2 — THE FIVE LEGAL VIOLATIONS

### VIOLATION 1: Breach of Duty of Care — Failure to Advise on Corporate Banking

**Legal Basis:**
- **Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982, s.13** — Implied term to carry out services with reasonable care and skill
- **Common Law Negligence** — *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd [1964] AC 465*
- **Bolam Standard** — *Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee [1957] 1 WLR 582*

**Analysis:**

The Bolam test requires that a professional meet the standard of a reasonably competent practitioner in their field. There is no competent UK company accountant who would permit the proceeds of a corporate property disposal to be received into a personal bank account without:

1. Explicit written client instruction advising against it
2. A clear tax warning that personal receipt destroys the MVL route
3. At minimum, ensuring a corporate account existed to receive the funds

Wincham's SIC code for the relevant entity is 82990 ("Other Business Support Services"), and Adrem carries code 69201 ("Accounting & Auditing"). Both operated in a professional advisory capacity in relation to Los Romeros Limited. Both owed a duty of care to Philip Harrison *and* to the company itself.

The failure to advise on the requirement for a corporate bank account is not a borderline professional judgement call — it is a **fundamental and elementary failure** that no competent professional in this field would make.

### VIOLATION 2: Breach of Fiduciary Duty as Company Secretary

**Legal Basis:**
- **Companies Act 2006, s.174** — Duty to exercise reasonable care, skill and diligence
- **Companies Act 2006, s.172** — Duty to act in the way most likely to promote the success of the company

**Analysis:**

Adrem Accounting Ltd was the duly appointed **Company Secretary of Los Romeros Limited** from 1 September 2025 — the period covering the sale completion (20 March 2026) and repatriation (~27 March 2026).

As company secretary, Adrem owed duties directly to **Los Romeros Limited as a legal entity**. Their duty was to protect the company's assets, ensure lawful operation of the company's affairs, and prevent the company's funds from being misdirected.

By routing corporate funds to a personal account — or failing to prevent this from happening — Adrem breached s.174 of the Companies Act 2006 in arguably the most fundamental way possible: by failing to protect the company's primary asset (the cash proceeds of its only business activity). This is not a procedural or administrative breach. It goes to the heart of the company secretary's function.

### VIOLATION 3: Breach of ICAEW / ACCA Client Money Regulations

**Legal Basis:**
- **ICAEW Clients' Money Regulations** (mandatory for ICAEW member firms)
- **ACCA Client Money Regulations** (mandatory for ACCA member firms)
- **ICAEW Code of Ethics** — Sections on Integrity, Objectivity, and Professional Behaviour

**Analysis:**

The ICAEW Clients' Money Regulations impose the following absolute obligations on member firms:

| Obligation | Standard | Breach? |
|---|---|---|
| Segregation of client funds | Client money must be kept separate from the firm's own money AND from the client's personal money | Likely Yes |
| Designated client account | Money must be paid only to a named, designated account for the correct legal entity | Likely Yes |
| No general banking facility | Client accounts must not be used as a general banking facility | Likely Yes |
| Written authorisation | Any movement of funds requires explicit written instruction from the correct authorised officer | Requires investigation |
| Record keeping | Five-weekly reconciliation of client accounts mandatory | Likely breach |

The ICAEW explicitly states: *"It is a fundamental breach of professional regulations to mix client funds with personal or office funds."* Routing Los Romeros Limited's corporate funds through Philip Harrison's personal account is precisely that breach.

### VIOLATION 4: Triggering Catastrophic UK Tax Consequences — The Destruction of the MVL Route

**Legal Basis:**
- **Corporation Tax Act 2009**
- **Corporation Tax Act 2010, s.455** — Tax on loans to participators
- **Income Tax (Trading and Other Income) Act 2005, Part 4** — Dividend income
- **HMRC Guidance CTM15000 series** — Tax treatment of company distributions

**Analysis:**

This is the most financially damaging consequence of Wincham's failure — and it represents the largest new head of damages in this case.

The correct method of distributing the sale proceeds from Los Romeros Limited to Philip Harrison is via a **Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL)**. Under this route:

- The distribution is a **capital payment** subject to **Capital Gains Tax** at 18–24%
- Philip Harrison's **CGT allowance** (£3,000 for 2025/26) can be applied
- **Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR)** is available against the Spanish CGT (Modelo 210) already paid

**If the funds arrived in Philip Harrison's personal account instead, HMRC will treat this as one of the following — neither of which is the intended MVL capital distribution:**

#### Scenario A — Treatment as an Informal Dividend

| Tax Metric | MVL Route (Correct) | Informal Dividend (Wrong) |
|---|---|---|
| Rate on basic rate band | **18% CGT** | 8.75% dividend tax |
| Rate on higher rate band | **24% CGT** | **33.75% dividend tax** ← punitive |
| Available allowances | £3,000 CGT exemption | £500 dividend allowance only |
| FTCR credit available? | **Yes** | Unclear / disputed |

On a distribution of this size (~£250,000–£265,000 sterling equivalent), almost the entire sum instantly falls into the higher rate band. The excess tax rate of 9.75% (33.75% − 24%) on approximately £215,000 = **~£20,963 in additional tax damage**. Add the FTCR credit lost (~£15,978) = **~£36,941 additional damage under Scenario A**.

#### Scenario B — Treatment as a Director's Loan (Section 455 CTA 2010)

If HMRC treats the personal receipt as an unsanctioned director's loan from the company:

- A **s.455 Corporation Tax charge** of **33.75%** of the outstanding loan balance applies
- This charge is **immediate** if not repaid within 9 months of the company year-end
- Los Romeros Limited is still an active company — this charge is live and accruing
- **Estimated additional tax damage:** 33.75% × ~£265,000 = **~£89,438** (reclaimable in principle upon MVL, but creates immediate cash pressure and penalty exposure)

> [!WARNING]
> **The MVL route is not necessarily dead** — but it is severely complicated. If the funds have arrived in Philip Harrison's personal account, urgent advice from an independent UK CTA is required immediately to determine whether a retrospective corporate account correction is possible. **Philip Harrison must not spend any of this money until this is resolved.**

### VIOLATION 5: Potential Exposure Under the Fraud Act 2006

**Legal Basis:**
- **Fraud Act 2006, s.4** — Fraud by Abuse of Position

**Analysis:**

Section 4 of the Fraud Act 2006 provides that a person commits fraud if they:

1. **Occupy a position** in which they are expected to safeguard, or not to act against, the financial interests of another person
2. **Dishonestly abuse** that position
3. Intend, by means of the abuse, to **make a gain** for themselves or another, or to **cause loss** to another

Wincham/Adrem, as company secretary and professional advisers, occupied exactly such a position of trust in relation to Los Romeros Limited and Philip Harrison. Whether the s.4 threshold of *dishonesty* is met depends on facts not yet established — specifically, whether they *knowingly* directed the Spanish notary to wire funds to a personal account in full awareness that this bypassed the corporate structure.

> [!CAUTION]
> **Criminal referral is premature at this stage.** This analysis does not assert that Wincham/Adrem acted dishonestly. However, if investigation reveals that they deliberately directed the Spanish notary to wire funds to a personal account without Philip Harrison's informed written consent, and in full knowledge of the corporate separation principle, s.4 Fraud Act 2006 exposure should be flagged to the instructed solicitor for formal consideration. A referral to **Action Fraud** (actionfraud.police.uk) may become appropriate.
