## 2. WHO SETS THE ITP RATE? LEGAL GOVERNANCE

### 2.1 The Legal Framework

ITP (Impuesto de Transmisiones Patrimoniales) is governed by:

- **National framework:** Real Decreto Legislativo 1/1993, de 24 de septiembre — *Texto Refundido de la Ley del Impuesto sobre Transmisiones Patrimoniales y Actos Jurídicos Documentados*
- **Regional devolution:** Under Spain's constitutional settlement (Constitución Española 1978, Arts 133–156), tax-raising powers were devolved to the **Comunidades Autónomas** (Autonomous Communities — equivalent to devolved regions/nations in the UK)
- **Canary Islands special status:** The Canary Islands has additional fiscal autonomy under the **Régimen Económico y Fiscal de Canarias (REF)** — a special tax regime recognised since Spain's accession to the EEC in 1986, and codified in **Ley 19/1994 de 6 de julio de modificación del Régimen Económico y Fiscal de Canarias**

### 2.2 Who Classifies/Sets the Rate?

| Level | Body | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **National (Estado)** | Cortes Generales / Ministerio de Hacienda | Sets the default "supletory" rate (6% nationally) and the legal framework via RDL 1/1993 |
| **Regional (Comunidad Autónoma)** | Each regional parliament (e.g., Parlamento de Canarias) | **Sets the actual operative ITP rate** for property transfers in their territory. They can set rates above or below the national default |
| **Local (Municipio)** | Municipal councils | Do NOT set ITP. They collect Plusvalía Municipal (IIVTNU) on land value gain — a separate tax |

**In plain terms:** ITP is a **regional (Autonomous Community) tax**. Each of Spain's 17 autonomous communities + 2 autonomous cities (Ceuta and Melilla) sets its own rate. The national government only acts as a fallback if a region fails to legislate. The Canary Islands has exercised this power and set ITP at **6.5%** (standard rate for resale residential property).

## 5. MULTIPLE LAYERS OF FALSE ADVERTISING

### Layer 1 — Wrong Rate for Wrong Region

Wincham marketed a saving based on mainland Spain ITP rates (7–10%, or possibly using a combined rate figure of up to 16.5%) to buyers of **Canary Islands property**, where the actual ITP rate is only **6.5%**. This is a straightforward geographical misrepresentation.

### Layer 2 — The 16.5% Figure Cannot Be Justified

No single Spanish autonomous community has an ITP rate of 16.5%. The figure may derive from:
- (a) Adding ITP + AJD (Stamp Duty) + notary costs together and calling it "the transfer tax" — misleading framing
- (b) Using outdated pre-devolution figures
- (c) Cherry-picking the highest combined property transaction cost across multiple taxes and presenting it as a single "tax" being avoided

In all cases, presenting 16.5% as the applicable "transfer tax" to a Lanzarote buyer is a **material misrepresentation**.

### Layer 3 — The Scheme May Not Have Avoided ITP at All

Spanish tax law requires ITP on the transfer of any economic interest in Spanish property. The Spanish Tax Authority's position is that:
- Transferring property into a corporate vehicle is itself a taxable event
- Subsequent sale of the company shares may attract ITP/AJD anyway
- HMRC Hacienda has issued rulings against similar "share transfer" schemes

If this is correct, **Wincham's clients paid Wincham's fees and got no ITP saving whatsoever** — while being sold on a false promise of up to 16.5% savings.

### Layer 4 — The Ongoing Cost Was Not Disclosed

The archived winchamukcompany.com page claims **"No 3% annual Off-Shore Company Tax"** as a benefit. Post-Brexit, this protection collapsed. But even pre-Brexit, there were annual compliance costs — company secretarial fees, UK filing fees, Spanish fiscal representation fees — that eroded or eliminated the claimed tax saving over time.
