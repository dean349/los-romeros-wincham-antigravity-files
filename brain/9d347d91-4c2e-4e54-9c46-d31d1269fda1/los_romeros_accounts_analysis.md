### SECTION 4: CREDITORS — AMOUNTS FALLING DUE AFTER MORE THAN ONE YEAR (Note 6)

#### ▸ Directors' Loan Account — **(£25,069)** (2025) | **(£19,373)** (2024)

> [!CAUTION]
> **THIS IS THE KEY LINE. THIS IS WHERE YOUR FATHER'S MONEY WENT.**

**What is a Director's Loan Account?**
A Director's Loan Account (DLA) records money that flows between a company and its director(s). It can work **two ways**:
1. **Director owes the company** — the director took money out (debit balance — a company asset)
2. **Company owes the director** — the director put money *into* the company (credit balance — a company liability)

In this case, the DLA is shown as a **liability** (a debt the company owes), which means **the company owes Philip Harrison money**. Specifically, it shows the company owes **£25,069**. The fact it's under "Creditors After More Than One Year" means it's not expected to be repaid within 12 months.

## 🔍 The Director's Loan — Deep Forensic Analysis

**Philip Harrison does not owe the company money. The company owes Philip Harrison £25,069.**

So where did this debt come from? Let's trace it.

### The Core Mechanism: How Fees Were Paid

When your father bought into this arrangement, **he never directly paid Wincham's fees**. Instead, the mechanics almost certainly worked like this:

1. **Wincham invoices the company** (Los Romeros Limited) for management services, accounting, and registered office fees each year.
2. The company has **no cash** — it has only the property, and the property generates no rental income.
3. So to **pay Wincham's fees**, someone has to put cash into the company. That person is the director — Philip Harrison.
4. Every time your father **transferred money into the Los Romeros company account to pay Wincham's fees**, that money was recorded as a **loan from the director to the company**.
5. Over years and years, these loans accumulated — each year's fee payment adding to the running total.

This is the Director's Loan Account. **It is a record of every pound your father has paid into the company on its behalf.**

> - **Back-billing**: Adrem billing for prior years' services in one go
> - **Winding-down fees**: Fees related to preparation for the property sale
> - **Unexplained management charges** not previously disclosed
> - **Accounting reclassification**: Prior balances reclassified into the DLA

> [!CAUTION]
> The **£25,069 balance is entirely inconsistent** with routine annual fees of ~£840 over 5–6 years (which would total only ~£4,200–£5,000). **This means the majority of the £25,069 was either:**
> 1. **Inherited from before the Harrisons bought the company** (ie, the previous owners left a director's loan in the company), OR
> 2. **Comprised of charges over and above the headline annual fee**, OR
> 3. **A combination of both**.

### Critical Question: Pre-Acquisition Balance

When your father acquired Los Romeros Limited in 2019, the company had **pre-existing financial history going back to 2010**. The previous owners (the Stockwell family) may have had their own Director's Loan Account. When the Harrisons acquired the company, either:
- That prior DLA was settled and cleared (as it should have been in any proper acquisition), OR
- **It was transferred across as part of the acquisition**, effectively meaning your father inherited someone else's accumulated charges.

**This needs to be investigated urgently.** Request the 2019 and 2020 accounts from Wincham/Adrem and compare the opening DLA balance from when your father first appeared as director.

#### ▸ Profit and Loss Account (Retained Earnings) — **(£25,689)** (2025) | **(£19,969)** (2024)

**What is it?**
This is the **cumulative profit or loss** the company has made since it started trading — or more precisely, since the Harrisons took over. The negative figure means the company has **accumulated losses of £25,689**.

**Where did these losses come from?**
The company has no rental income and no other trading income. Its only expenses are the annual accountancy fees and any other charges. Each year, these charges flow through as losses on the Profit & Loss account.

The loss increased by **£5,720** (from £19,969 to £25,689) — once again, almost perfectly matching the £5,696 increase in the Director's Loan. This is arithmetically consistent: the director funds the fees → fees are charged as expenses → expenses create a P&L loss.

> [!NOTE]
> The £25,689 accumulated loss is an **accounting entry** — it does not mean your father has "lost" £25,689 in cash. It records that the company has spent that amount on fees and has no revenue to offset it. The real wealth sits in the property (and now, in the sale proceeds).

## The Director's Loan — What You Must Do Now

Given the sale completed in March 2026 and proceeds have arrived in the UK, here is the critical position on the DLA:

### 1. The £25,069 Must Be Repaid to Philip as Part of the MVL
When the Members' Voluntary Liquidation is executed, **Philip Harrison is a creditor of the company to the tune of £25,069**. He must be repaid this amount **before** the remaining assets are distributed. This is separate from — and in priority to — any capital distribution to shareholders.

### 2. Demand a Full Itemised Breakdown Immediately
Write to Adrem Accounting Ltd and demand a **complete written breakdown** of every transaction that makes up the £25,069. They must be able to account for every penny. If they cannot, this is evidence of accounting malpractice.

### 3. Investigate the Pre-Acquisition Balance
Pull the **2019 and 2020 accounts** and check what the Director's Loan balance was at:
- Year ending August 2019 (before the Harrisons bought it)
- Year ending August 2020 (first full year of Harrison ownership)

If there was already a balance when your father acquired the company (ie, a pre-existing loan in the Stockwells' names), it should have been **settled at the point of sale**. If it wasn't, Wincham may have transferred a pre-existing liability onto your father without disclosure.

### 4. The S.455 Corporation Tax Risk
Because the DLA is shown as money **owed TO the director**, and not by the director, there is **no s.455 Corporation Tax charge** at present. S.455 only applies when a director has borrowed from the company, not the other way around. This is good news — provided the accounting is correct and your father genuinely lent money in (rather than took it out).
