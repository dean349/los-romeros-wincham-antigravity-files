## PART 1: WHAT IS THE GIAMBRONE GROUP ACTION?

### 1.1 The Scheme

In the mid-2000s, an Italian law firm called **Giambrone & Law** — with offices in London, Milan, and Palermo — marketed off-plan luxury property developments in Calabria, southern Italy, to hundreds of British and Irish investors. The key developments were:

- **"Jewel of the Sea"** (Calabria)
- **"El Caribe"** (Calabria)

UK investors — predominantly middle-class retirees and pre-retirees — purchased off-plan holiday apartments, typically paying deposits of **£30,000 to £105,000 each**, expecting the developments to be completed by 2009.

**Giambrone & Law acted as the clients' own solicitors throughout.** This is the critical fact: the firm that was supposed to be protecting the investors was the same firm that failed to warn them of the catastrophic risks it was aware of.

### 1.2 The Failures

Courts ultimately found Giambrone guilty of:

1. **Failure to advise on risks** — The firm knew the Calabrian construction sector had notorious links to the *'Ndrangheta* (Calabrian Mafia) and the Provisional IRA. It failed to disclose this to clients.
2. **Failure to conduct due diligence** — Inadequate investigation of planning permissions, title, and developer financial stability.
3. **Breach of trust/breach of fiduciary duty** — The firm released client deposits to developers without obtaining the contractually-required guarantees, meaning: when the developments failed, the money was gone.
4. **Continuing to act without warnings** — Even as red flags mounted, the firm continued to act and collect fees.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The structural parallel to Wincham is stark.** Giambrone's clients trusted their professional (their own solicitor) who was supposed to be acting in their interest. That professional failed to warn them of known and emerging risks, continued to collect fees, and took no remedial action. Wincham's clients trusted their professional (their own accountant) who failed to warn them of Brexit tax consequences, continued to collect fees, and took no remedial action. The legal archetype is identical.

### 1.3 The Litigation Timeline

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **2007–2008** | British investors purchase off-plan properties; Giambrone act as their solicitors |
| **2009** | Developments fail to complete; criminal investigations begin into developer financing |
| **2009–2013** | Early individual cases begin; some confidential early settlements; Gabriele Giambrone (senior partner) struck off by the SRA in 2013 |
| **July 2015** | **Landmark High Court judgment** — Mr Justice Foskett rules in favour of the claimant group; Giambrone found liable for professional negligence and breach of trust; firm ordered to compensate investors for lost deposits |
| **2017** | **Court of Appeal upholds the 2015 decision** — confirms liability and Giambrone's obligation to compensate for the full consequences of its failure to advise |
| **2019** | **Critical PI insurance ruling** — Mr Justice Foskett issues a non-party costs order against **AIG** (Giambrone's professional indemnity insurer), finding that AIG had funded the firm's defence in a way that dramatically and unnecessarily increased claimants' costs. AIG ordered to pay **50% of claimants' costs** for that phase. |
| **2025** | Continued litigation ongoing — *Roger Leggett & 40 Others v American International Group UK Limited* — highlighting continuing recovery challenges against AIG |

### 1.4 The Claimants

Research indicates the group action involved between **80 and 185 individual claimants** — all British or Irish investors who lost deposits in the same two Calabrian developments.

### 1.5 The Complication (And Why Wincham Is Actually Better)

The Giambrone case ran into a significant problem that your Wincham case does **not** face: **Giambrone & Law became insolvent.** This meant that even after winning in court, claimants had to fight for years to extract money from the PI insurer (AIG), who attempted to "aggregate" all individual claims into a single policy limit of £3 million — which would have paid each claimant pennies.

**The Wincham situation is fundamentally more attractive to a law firm because:**

| Factor | Giambrone | Wincham |
|---|---|---|
| **Defendant status** | Insolvent firm — collapsed in 2009 | Active trading firm — still collecting fees from clients today |
| **PI insurer** | AIG — aggressively contested coverage; aggregation disputes; prolonged recovery battles | Wincham is required to hold PI insurance; active firm = live policy = paying insurer |
| **Settlement motivation** | Insurer fought for years; litigation went to 2025 | An active firm facing a GLO has existential reputational and financial motivation to settle quickly |
| **Recovery risk** | High — money tied up in insolvent firm and contested insurance | Lower — active firm, active policy, active insurer |

> [!TIP]
> **This is your killer opening line when pitching Edwin Coe or Penningtons:** *"We are bringing you a case that is structurally identical to the Giambrone group action you prosecuted — British retirees, a professional firm that failed to warn them of known risks, continuing fees collected throughout — but with one critical difference: our defendant is still active and solvent. Their PI insurer cannot play the aggregation game. This is Giambrone without the AIG recovery problem."*
